# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Fellow travelers: indie-web readers and game- or fantasy-curious folks who
follow a personal journey rather than a content feed. They arrive from
micro.blog timelines, blogrolls ("Allied Mages"), and word of mouth, usually
unhurried and reading for atmosphere as much as substance. They are here for
the person behind the persona, not for a product. Many read on mobile; some
return often enough to recognize recurring in-world furniture.

The job to be done: drop into an ongoing chronicle, feel transported, read a
post comfortably, and leave with a sense of having visited somewhere rather
than browsed something.

## Product Purpose

The Game Mage is a personal blog: one mage documenting the real journey from
where the author started to founding an indie game studio. "Following a dream
and die trying" is the in-world phrasing of exactly that arc, not a vague
quest. The site exists to make that chronicle feel like a place worth being.
Success is not engagement metrics; it is that a first-time reader immediately
understands this is a crafted, light-hearted world with a voice, and a
returning reader feels continuity. The author publishes pseudonymously as
Contrariwise; the persona is the public identity and must stay intact
everywhere.

The long-horizon ambition: the site itself slowly grows into a game. A small
game, perhaps eventually a large one, form deliberately undecided, embroidered
into the fabric of a site that works first and foremost as a top-tier
reading experience. This is directional intent, not a roadmap and not a
shipped feature; nothing here authorizes building game functionality yet. Its
only operative consequence today is that the keystone below is non-negotiable
from the very first commit: the embroidered game is reachable only on a
foundation where every small thing is already right.

## Brand Personality

Light, fun, playful, magical. Three words: **playful, magical, meticulous**.
The voice is an RPG mage's in-world narration applied to ordinary blog
mechanics, never winking too hard at the bit. The emotional target is the
spiritual ilk of Disney / Disneyland: not their visual branding, but the
*reason* it feels magical. Disney sweats the small stuff and gets every tiny
thing right 100% of the time, and the magic is precisely that no one ever
consciously notices the craft while it is working. Wonder that arrives as
lightness and ease, not awe at a spectacle. Playful but not jokey; the persona
is sincere, not a costume.

The author's creative North Stars are Walt Disney, Jim Henson, and Steve Jobs.
What they share, and what this site inherits: a relentless pursuit of quality
in the craft, delighting people, authenticity, and doing the right thing even
when it is hard. Layered on top is the author's own brand, leaned into hard:
wizardry and game-craft. The site should not merely describe a journey in
indie gaming; it should *feel* like a game in its small moments, rewarding
curiosity with delight that is never load-bearing and never in the way.

## Anti-references

- **Corporate SaaS / startup landing.** No hero-metric templates, gradient
  CTAs, feature-card grids, or "modern clean" minimalism. The opposite of
  generic.
- **Cheesy fantasy kitsch.** No dragons-and-Comic-Sans, faux-parchment
  textures, Renaissance-faire clip art, or stock wizard imagery. The mage
  atmosphere is crafted through type, color, and language, never props.
- **Sterile dev blog.** Not monospace-on-white technical austerity.
  Personality and atmosphere outrank restraint-as-default.
- **Overdesigned / heavy.** No animation overload, decorative glassmorphism,
  or effects that fight readability. Restraint *within* the world.

## Design Principles

- **Sweat the small stuff (the keystone).** This is the Disney thesis and it
  outranks the rest. The magic is the cumulative effect of hundreds of small
  things being right 100% of the time, none of which the reader consciously
  notices while they work. Delight by invisible craft, never by visible
  spectacle. The interface is defined by the *absence* of friction and seams:
  nothing janky, nothing half-done, nothing that pulls you out of the world.
  Audit test: if a reader catches a delightful detail "happening" and thinks
  "oh, neat," it is slightly too loud. Quiet it until the magic is felt, not
  spotted.
- **The experience first; the game is embroidered in.** The site must work,
  foremost, as a top-tier reading experience at a Disney / Jobs level of
  polish. The eventual game grows *inside* the weave, discoverable and hidden,
  never a feature bolted on top and never degrading the base. Build every
  piece now as if the game is coming, because it is. A delightful moment that
  becomes load-bearing, or that gets in the reader's way, has broken this
  principle, not honored it.
- **The persona is load-bearing.** Every label is diegetic: navigation is the
  "Teleportation Circle," statuses are "Mystic Missives," pages are "scrolls,"
  posts are "Inscribed on." In-world naming over conventional UI vocabulary,
  always. Breaking character anywhere breaks the spell everywhere.
- **Wonder without kitsch.** Atmosphere comes from a committed palette,
  consistent voice, and typographic discipline, not from imagery or texture.
  The magic lives in the system, not in clip art.
- **Legibility is part of the spell.** Accessibility is not a tax on the
  aesthetic. The hyperlegible body font and motion-awareness are *how* the
  wonder reaches everyone; they are non-negotiable craft, not a compromise.
- **A journey, not a portfolio.** The site is an ongoing chronicle being
  inscribed, not a polished product pitch. Design should feel lived-in and
  continuous, never like a launch.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Every text and accent color must meet AA contrast against
the background it sits on. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion` for all transition
and motion effects. Keep Atkinson Hyperlegible (Next / Mono) as the legibility
backbone of running body text and code; reserve any decorative display face
for headings only, never for running text.
