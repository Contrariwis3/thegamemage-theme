On choosing the stock before the ink

There is a temptation, when a workshop is new, to reach first for the brightest pigments. The temptation is a trap. The stock decides everything: an ink that sings on one page mutters on another, and no amount of flourish recovers a sheet chosen carelessly. So we begin where the careful binder begins, with the page itself, held to the light and turned until it is right.

A reader should never notice this decision. That is the whole of the craft: the work that is felt and never spotted. If you catch a detail happening and think "oh, neat," it was, by a hair, too loud — and we quiet it until the magic is only felt. For a longer treatment, see the earlier note on invisible seams.

What the press taught us

Depth here is not borrowed from shadow. It is the honest depth of a printed plate: one ink laid beside another, a hairline ruled where a boundary is needed, and margin enough to breathe.

The plate has no glow. It was never lit. It was inked, by someone who cared, and set down exactly where it belongs.

The mono hand is reserved for the literal glyphs of the craft:

spell := bind(stock, ink)        # the page comes first
cast  := spell.press()           # flat, opaque, no halo
assert cast.legible              # legibility is part of the spell
  • The stock is dark by conviction, not by reflex.
  • The ink is flat, opaque, and committed.
  • The reading hand stays hyperlegible, always.
